# Preface

Digital health and care is now delivered by organisations that must be, all at once, safe like a hospital, iterative like a start-up, accountable like a government department, and collaborative like a coalition. No single existing playbook covers that combination. Software teams have agile and product management; the public sector has the GOV.UK and NHS service standards; large change has programme and portfolio management; the health system has improvement science and a deep workforce evidence base. Each is excellent in its lane. The problem leaders actually face is that their work spans all the lanes at once, every day.

This book is an attempt to assemble those disciplines into one coherent set of **ways of working** for a digital health and care organisation — and to do it honestly, naming trade-offs rather than selling a method.

### Why "ways of working" deserves a book

In most industries, how you organise work is an efficiency question. In health and care it is also a **safety** question (bad process harms patients), an **equity** question (services designed narrowly exclude the people who need them most), a **public-money** question (the funding is taxpayers' and finite), and a **trust** question (people share their most sensitive data with you). That raises the stakes on ordinary decisions — how work gets in the door, how it is triaged and prioritised, how teams are shaped, how change is led — to a level that justifies treating them as a discipline.

### What this book covers

The chapters move from foundations (what ways of working means, the operating model, evidence-driven decisions), through the flow of work (intake, triage, prioritisation) and the delivery lifecycle (discovery, and the discovery→alpha→beta→live path), into the modes you deliver in (product, project, programme, portfolio, and the ERP backbone), then the people who do the work (team structures, workforce planning, workforce strategy, and organisational development), the disciplines of change and innovation, the realities of partnership and government, and finally measurement and the visibility senior leaders need.

Every chapter is built the same way — thesis, why it matters in health and care, core concepts, best practices, a worked health example, failure modes, a maturity model, a checklist, and real sources — so you can assess where you are and decide what to do next without reading cover to cover.

### How to read it

Read Part I to fix the shared vocabulary, then treat the rest as a reference. If your problem is "too much work, no way to choose," start at Part II. If it is "our transformation keeps stalling," start with change (Chapter 8.5) and phasing innovation (Chapter 8.4). If it is "the board can't see what's really happening," start with Chapters 10.3 and 10.4. The cross-references will pull you to the neighbouring chapters you need.

### A note on grounding and scope

The centre of gravity is the UK's NHS and social care system, because that is where much of the best public guidance on these ways of working has been written and tested — the NHS Digital Service Manual, the GOV.UK Service Manual, NICE's Evidence Standards Framework, and the health-and-care workforce strategies among them. The principles, though, are not parochial: safety-critical, publicly-accountable, partnership-dependent health organisations everywhere face the same forces. Where the book cites frameworks, statistics or documents, it cites real, checkable ones; where the evidence is thin, it says so rather than inventing certainty. The aim throughout is a handbook you can act on, not admire.
