# Chapter 17 — Programme-Led Work

**In one sentence:** Programme-led work coordinates a set of related projects and products toward a strategic change that none could deliver alone, holding a clear line of sight from the work to the benefits it is meant to realise.

## Why this matters in health and care

The biggest changes in health and care are never a single project. Rolling out a shared care record across an [integrated care system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_care_system) (ICS), moving a region to a single [electronic patient record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_health_record), transforming an urgent-and-emergency-care pathway, or delivering a virtual-ward programme across multiple trusts — each of these is a coordinated bundle of projects, product teams, workforce change, estates work, and clinical redesign, aligned to one strategic outcome. Manage the pieces in isolation and they collide: incompatible data standards, duplicated spend, contradictory clinical workflows, and benefits that never actually materialise.

[Programme management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_management) exists to hold that coalition together and to keep it pointed at *outcomes and benefits*, not just outputs. This matters acutely in health and care because system-wide change touches patient safety across organisational boundaries, commits large sums of public money over several years, and depends on many autonomous organisations choosing to move in step. A programme provides the leadership, the shared target state, and the benefits discipline to make that happen — and, just as importantly, to know whether it is working.

The recurring failure it guards against is the programme that spends millions, delivers a pile of technical outputs, and yet changes nothing measurable for patients or staff. This chapter uses Managing Successful Programmes (MSP), the UK public sector's dominant framework, and shows how programme-led work coexists with the project-led (Chapter 16 — Project-Led Work) and product-led (Chapter 15 — Product-Led Work) modes it coordinates. Chapter 18 — Enterprise Portfolio & Project Management (EPPM) then explains how an organisation prioritises across many programmes.

## Core concepts

**Programme.** A temporary, flexible organisation created to coordinate, direct, and oversee a group of related projects and activities to deliver outcomes and benefits aligned to strategic objectives. A programme differs from a project in that it is defined by the *outcome and benefits* it seeks, and it manages the uncertainty of coordinating many moving parts, rather than delivering a single fixed output.

**MSP (Managing Successful Programmes).** The UK best-practice framework for managing transformational change through multiple related projects. It is organised around principles, themes, and a "transformational flow" of processes, and is designed for large, complex change where the end state is known in direction but the path involves many interdependent deliveries.

**Vision and blueprint.** The vision is the compelling picture of the future the programme will create. The **blueprint** makes it concrete: it describes the [target operating model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Target_operating_model) — the future state of processes, organisation, technology, information, and roles (often summarised as POTI). The blueprint is the reference point every project is steered toward, so the pieces add up to a coherent whole.

**[Benefits realisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benefits_realisation_management).** The heart of programme management. Benefits are the measurable improvements that justify the investment — reduced delayed discharges, fewer duplicated tests, faster diagnosis, lower administrative burden. MSP treats identifying, mapping, measuring, and actively managing benefits across the whole lifecycle as a core discipline, because outputs only create value when they are used to change how care is delivered.

**Tranches.** MSP breaks a programme into tranches — blocks of work ending at a major review point where a step of capability has been delivered and benefits realisation can begin. A tranche is a moment to demonstrate outcomes, reassess the blueprint against reality, and decide whether to continue, adjust, or stop, rather than merely a slice of the schedule.

**Programme board and SRO.** The **[Senior Responsible Owner (SRO)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_sponsor)** is the single individual accountable for the programme meeting its objectives and realising its benefits, with enough seniority and authority to lead and to make decisions. The SRO chairs a programme board — typically a business change manager, project leads, and senior stakeholders — that provides direction across organisational boundaries.

**Business change manager (BCM).** MSP's distinctive role: someone from the operational business (not the delivery team) accountable for embedding the change and realising the benefits on the ground. The BCM is why programmes deliver outcomes and not just outputs — projects hand over capability, the BCM turns it into benefit.

## Best practices

1. **Anchor the programme in a vision and a blueprint.** Define the strategic outcome and translate it into a concrete target operating model (processes, organisation, technology, information — POTI). Every constituent project and product is then steered toward the same future state, which is what stops a coalition of deliveries from fragmenting.

2. **Manage benefits, not just deliverables.** Build a benefits map linking each output to the outcomes and measurable benefits it should produce, with owners and baselines. Review benefits actively across the programme, and be willing to stop work that is not going to pay back — MSP's emphasis on benefits realisation is the discipline that separates a programme from an expensive to-do list.

3. **Structure the work into tranches with real review points.** Deliver capability in tranches, and at each tranche boundary demonstrate the benefits realised so far, re-test the blueprint against what you have learned, and make a genuine continue/adjust/stop decision. Early tranches should target visible quick wins to build stakeholder confidence for the harder later ones.

4. **Appoint an empowered SRO and a real programme board.** Name a single SRO with the seniority and authority to direct the programme and decide across organisational boundaries, and convene a board that genuinely represents the affected organisations. Programmes fail when accountability is diffuse or the SRO cannot actually make cross-boundary decisions stick.

5. **Put business change managers in the operational business.** Assign BCMs from the frontline organisations to own adoption and benefit realisation where care is delivered. Capability delivered but not adopted realises no benefit; the BCM role is the mechanism that closes that gap.

6. **Coordinate — do not micromanage — the constituent projects and products.** The programme sets the blueprint, the interdependencies, the standards, and the benefits; it lets projects and product teams deliver within that frame. It manages the seams — shared data standards, information governance, sequencing, and shared infrastructure — not the internal detail of each delivery.

7. **Manage cross-cutting risk, dependency, and stakeholders relentlessly.** The value a programme adds over standalone projects is largely in managing what happens *between* them. Maintain a programme-level view of interdependencies and shared risks, and invest heavily in [stakeholder engagement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_management) across autonomous organisations, since alignment is voluntary, not commanded.

8. **Close the programme when the change is embedded, and hand ongoing services to product teams.** A programme is temporary. When the blueprint is achieved and benefits are being realised, close it deliberately, transfer any enduring services to durable product teams (Chapter 15), and keep tracking benefits into business-as-usual — many benefits accrue after the programme ends.

## Questions to discuss with your team

1. **Does our senior responsible owner actually have the authority to make decisions stick across sovereign partner organisations, or only the accountability when they do not?**
   Programme-led work lives or dies on cross-boundary authority, because the change spans autonomous trusts, primary care, community, mental health, and social care that cannot simply be ordered to move in step. The characteristic failure is a named SRO who carries the personal, public accountability for benefits but lacks the standing to compel a reluctant partner to adopt a shared data standard or change a clinical workflow — accountability without authority. The debate should be candid about where real power sits in the system: whether the programme board genuinely represents the affected organisations or is a reporting forum, whether the SRO is senior enough that their decisions are honoured across sovereign boundaries, and what happens when one partner declines to move. Consider a recent cross-organisation decision and trace whether it actually held. A good answer names who the SRO is and tests their authority against a concrete cross-boundary conflict, is honest about the organisations that can effectively opt out, and treats a representative board and embedded business change managers as load-bearing mechanisms for voluntary alignment rather than governance decoration.

2. **How will we know this programme has realised benefits and not merely delivered outputs — and who, in the operational business, is accountable for turning capability into benefit?**
   Output theatre is the recurring pathology the chapter warns against: a programme spends millions, produces a pile of interoperability platforms and integrations, and yet nothing measurable changes for patients or staff. The distinction that prevents it is the business change manager — someone from the frontline operation, not the delivery team, accountable for embedding the change and realising benefit where care is delivered — so the question forces the team to name those people and confirm they exist. The tension is that delivery is visible and satisfying while adoption is slow, unglamorous, and easy to assume will just happen; a shared care record buried behind an extra login realises no benefit however well it is built. Bring the benefits map to the discussion and stress-test whether each output is linked to an owned, baselined, measurable benefit (fewer duplicated tests, reduced delayed discharges) or whether benefits are merely asserted. An honest answer names a BCM in each partner organisation, admits where benefits are currently unowned or unmeasured, and accepts that some benefits accrue only after the programme closes and must be tracked into business-as-usual.

3. **Are we tranching this programme to bank early benefits and create real continue/adjust/stop points, or drifting toward a big-bang delivery that defers all the risk to one late moment?**
   Tranches are what separate a programme from an expensive frozen plan: each tranche boundary should deliver a step of capability, demonstrate benefits realised so far, re-test the blueprint against what has actually been learned, and support a genuine decision to continue, adjust, or stop. The health and care temptation is to sequence for engineering convenience and defer visible value — connecting every trust before anyone can use anything — which loads all the risk into a single late delivery and leaves the board with no evidence to defend continued investment through fiscal and political cycles. The debate should examine whether early tranches target deliberate quick wins that build stakeholder confidence for the harder later work, and whether the review points are real decisions or schedule checkpoints. Consider what the first demonstrable benefit is, how soon it lands, and what the board would actually do at a tranche review if adoption lagged or the blueprint proved wrong. A good answer describes concrete tranches with early, measurable wins, treats each boundary as a real go/no-go with the blueprint genuinely open to revision, and is honest about any pressure to defer value to the end.

## In practice: a health & care example

An ICS sets out to deliver a shared care record giving clinicians across acute, community, mental health, primary care, and social care a unified view of a person. No single project can deliver this: it spans multiple trusts, several source systems, information-governance agreements, clinical-workflow redesign, and public engagement. It is coordinated as an MSP programme.

The SRO — an ICS executive with genuine cross-system authority — chairs a programme board with representatives from each partner organisation and named business change managers in each. The programme sets a vision ("the right information, about the right person, available to the right clinician, at the point of care") and a blueprint describing the target operating model: the data-sharing architecture and standards ([HL7 FHIR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_Healthcare_Interoperability_Resources)-based [interoperability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interoperability)), the information-governance framework, the consent model, and the redesigned clinical workflows.

The work is organised into tranches. Tranche one connects two willing acute trusts and delivers a read-only record, a deliberate quick win that proves the approach and lets the board demonstrate an early benefit — a measurable reduction in duplicated tests — to build confidence. At the tranche-one review, the board learns that clinician adoption lags where the record is buried in an extra login, so it adjusts the blueprint to prioritise [single-sign-on](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_sign-on) integration before widening rollout.

Underneath the programme, the modes coexist. The interoperability platform is run by a **durable product team** (Chapter 15) that keeps improving it across the whole programme and beyond. Each trust's connection is a bounded **project** (Chapter 16) with its own business case and go-live. The programme itself coordinates the blueprint, the standards, the interdependencies, and — through the BCMs — the benefits realisation. When the blueprint is achieved and duplicated-testing and delayed-discharge benefits are flowing, the programme closes; the platform product team endures, and benefit tracking continues into business-as-usual.

## Three sector lenses

### Startup

Few startups run programmes in the MSP sense — they lack the multi-organisation scope that defines one — but a scale-up deploying across several trusts effectively becomes a programme *participant*. Its realistic role is as a supplier inside someone else's programme: aligning its product to the programme blueprint, hitting tranche milestones, and feeding the benefits case that the SRO must evidence. Funding and survival depend on the programme continuing, so the startup treats tranche reviews as existential go/no-go points and invests in the business change managers' success, because adoption on the ground is what renews its contract. The discipline it must borrow is benefits language — framing its value as realised outcomes, not features shipped.

### Enterprise

An ICS or large trust is the natural home of programme-led work: shared care records, EPR convergence, and pathway transformation are inherently multi-organisation and multi-year. The hard part is authority — the SRO must make decisions stick across sovereign partner organisations that cannot simply be ordered, so a genuinely representative programme board and business change managers embedded in each partner are load-bearing, not decorative. Funding is blended (system transformation money, trust capital, national allocations), which makes the benefits map and tranche reviews essential for defending continued investment to system leaders and regulators. The recurring risk at this scale is output theatre: technical delivery with no one accountable for turning it into benefit.

### Government

At national scale, programmes carry the heaviest governance — they typically enter the Government Major Projects Portfolio, undergo Infrastructure and Projects Authority assurance and gateway reviews, and answer to ministers, the Public Accounts Committee, and the National Audit Office. The SRO is personally and publicly accountable for benefits realisation across the whole system, and blueprints must reconcile national standards (interoperability, information governance, the NHS App as a common front door) with local autonomy. Funding runs through multi-year Spending Review settlements, so tranching and benefits evidence are the mechanism for sustaining or halting investment across fiscal and political cycles. The stakes are population-scale: a national programme's benefits — or its failure — are measured in millions of patients and billions of pounds.

## Common failure modes

- **Output theatre.** The programme delivers technical outputs but no one owns turning them into benefits, so nothing changes for patients. Fix: benefits maps with owners, and BCMs in the operational business.
- **A programme that is just a big project.** Fixed-scope, output-focused governance applied to genuinely uncertain, multi-organisation change. Fix: manage by outcomes/benefits and tranches, not a single frozen plan.
- **A powerless SRO.** Accountability without authority to decide across boundaries. Fix: appoint an SRO senior enough to make cross-system decisions stick, backed by a representative board.
- **No blueprint / target state.** Projects optimise locally and the pieces never cohere. Fix: define and maintain a POTI blueprint everything steers toward.
- **Big-bang instead of tranches.** All the risk deferred to one late delivery. Fix: tranche the work, bank early benefits, and re-plan at review points.
- **Programme that never closes.** It becomes a permanent bureaucracy long after the change is embedded. Fix: close deliberately, hand enduring services to product teams, keep tracking benefits in BAU.

## Maturity model

| Dimension | Initial | Developing | Defined | Optimising |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic alignment | Projects run independently, no shared vision | Vision stated but no blueprint | Vision + POTI blueprint steer all deliveries | Blueprint continuously re-tested and adjusted at reviews |
| Benefits | Benefits assumed, never measured | Benefits listed but unowned | Benefits mapped, owned, and baselined | Benefits actively realised and tracked into BAU |
| Structure | One big undifferentiated effort | Loosely grouped projects | Tranches with real review/decision points | Early quick wins fund confidence for later tranches |
| Leadership | No SRO; diffuse accountability | SRO named but under-empowered | Empowered SRO + representative board + BCMs | Cross-boundary decisions made and embedded routinely |
| Adoption | Capability delivered, adoption ignored | Adoption hoped for | BCMs own adoption in the business | Change embedded; benefits accrue after close |

## Checklist

- [ ] Is this genuinely multi-project, multi-organisation change toward one strategic outcome?
- [ ] Is there a clear vision and a concrete blueprint (target operating model / POTI)?
- [ ] Is there a benefits map linking outputs to measurable, owned, baselined benefits?
- [ ] Is the work structured into tranches with real continue/adjust/stop review points?
- [ ] Is there an empowered SRO with authority to decide across organisational boundaries?
- [ ] Does the programme board genuinely represent the affected organisations?
- [ ] Are business change managers embedded in the operational business to own adoption?
- [ ] Are cross-cutting standards, interdependencies, and shared risks managed at programme level?
- [ ] Do product-led, project-led, and programme-led modes coexist with clear roles (Chapters 15–10)?
- [ ] Is there a deliberate close, with enduring services handed to durable product teams and benefits tracked into BAU?
