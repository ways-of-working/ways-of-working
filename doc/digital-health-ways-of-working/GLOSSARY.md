# Glossary

Definitions of the key terms used throughout *Ways of Working for Digital Health & Care*. Where a term has a good encyclopaedic entry, a Wikipedia link is provided for further reading. Cross-references point to the chapter where the term is developed most fully.

## A

**Accessibility** — Designing services so people with disabilities can use them; in UK public services, measured against the [Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_Content_Accessibility_Guidelines) and a legal duty. See Chapters 1.9, 3.0.

**ADKAR** — Prosci's individual change model: Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. A people-centred complement to organizational change models. See Chapter 8.1.

**Agile** — A family of iterative, incremental ways of working that deliver value in small increments and adapt to feedback. See [Agile software development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agile_software_development). See Chapters 1.0, 3.5.

**Alert fatigue** — Desensitization caused by too many low-value safety alerts, so clinicians begin to override important and trivial warnings alike. See [Alarm fatigue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alarm_fatigue). See Chapters 4.7, 4.8.

**Alpha** — The lifecycle phase where a team tests solutions to the problems found in discovery, usually with throwaway prototypes. See Chapter 3.5.

**Arden syntax** — A standard for encoding shareable medical knowledge as executable rules (Medical Logic Modules) for clinical decision support. See [Arden syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arden_syntax). See Chapter 4.7.

**Architecture decision record (ADR)** — A short document capturing one significant design decision, its context and its consequences, kept close to the code. See Chapter 10.3.

**Artificial intelligence (AI)** — Computer systems performing tasks that normally require human intelligence; in health, increasingly delivered through machine learning. See [Artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence). See Chapter 8.4.

**Assisted digital** — Support for people who cannot use a digital service independently, so they are not excluded. See Chapter 1.9.

**Asynchronous consultation (e-consultation)** — Store-and-forward clinical contact where patient and clinician are not present at the same time. See Chapter 3.11.

**Autoscaling** — Automatic adjustment of cloud capacity to match demand, a core lever for cutting idle spend. See [Autoscaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoscaling). See Chapter 6.5.

**Airlock (output checking)** — In a Secure Data Environment, the holding area and human review gate through which analytical results pass, checked for disclosure risk, before release. See Chapter 4.10.

## B

**Budget impact analysis** — An estimate of the total cash cost of adopting an intervention across a health system; something can be cost-effective yet unaffordable in-year. See Chapter 6.1.

**Behaviour Change Wheel / COM-B** — A framework explaining behaviour through Capability, Opportunity and Motivation, used to design adoption interventions. See Chapter 8.3.

**Big bang adoption** — Switching an organization onto a new system on a single cut-over date, with no parallel-running period. See [Big bang adoption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_bang_adoption). See Chapter 4.6.

**Business continuity** — Keeping critical services running, and recovering them, through disruption. See [Business continuity planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_continuity_planning). See Chapter 3.9.

**Business intelligence** — Tools and practices that turn data into reporting and insight for decisions. See [Business intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_intelligence). See Chapter 4.5.

**Balanced scorecard** — A strategic performance framework balancing financial, customer, internal-process, and learning-and-growth measures. See [Balanced scorecard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balanced_scorecard). See Chapter 10.0.

**Benefits realization** — The discipline of identifying, planning, measuring and actually achieving the benefits a change is meant to deliver. See [Benefits realisation management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benefits_realisation_management). See Chapters 5.2, 5.3, 8.1.

**Beta** — The lifecycle phase where a team builds and iterates a working service with real users, first privately then publicly. See Chapter 3.5.

**Bus factor** — The number of people who would have to be lost before a system or service could no longer be run or understood; a measure of key-person risk. See [Bus factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_factor). See Chapter 10.3.

**Business case** — The justification for an investment, in the UK public sector usually the [Five Case Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_case) (strategic, economic, commercial, financial, management). See Chapters 6.0, 9.1.

**Bioinformatics** — The computational pipelines that turn raw genomic sequence into interpreted, clinically actionable variants. See [Bioinformatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioinformatics). See Chapter 4.11.

## C

**Cost–benefit analysis (CBA)** — An economic evaluation that expresses both costs and health benefits in monetary terms. See [Cost–benefit analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93benefit_analysis). See Chapter 6.1.

**Care Quality Commission (CQC)** — The independent regulator of health and adult social care in England, whose remit includes digitally-enabled and well-led care. See Chapter 1.5.

**Chargeback** — Recovering technology cost from the consuming team's own budget, as opposed to showback, which only reports it. See [Cost allocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_allocation). See Chapter 6.5.

**Chief Clinical Information Officer (CCIO)** — A senior clinician accountable for the clinical design, safety and adoption of an organization's IT; with the Chief Nursing Information Officer (CNIO), leads clinical informatics. See [Chief medical informatics officer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chief_medical_informatics_officer). See Chapters 4.6, 4.7.

**Clinical decision support (CDS / CDSS)** — Health IT that delivers person-specific, filtered knowledge to a clinician at the point of care to improve a decision. See [Clinical decision support system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clinical_decision_support_system). See Chapter 4.7.

**Clinical engineering** — The discipline (also biomedical engineering) that manages medical devices across their lifecycle — inventory, maintenance, safety and decommissioning. See Chapter 4.4.

**Clinical informatics** — The discipline joining clinical practice, information and technology; the professional home of the CCIO and CNIO. See [Health informatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_informatics). See Chapter 4.7.

**Closed-loop medication administration** — An unbroken electronic chain from prescribing to dispensing to barcode-verified bedside administration, enforcing the "five rights" of medication. See Chapter 4.8.

**Committed-use / reserved capacity** — Discounted cloud pricing exchanged for a one- or three-year commitment to a baseline level of usage. See Chapter 6.5.

**Computerized physician order entry (CPOE)** — Electronic entry of clinical orders (tests, drugs, referrals) by the ordering clinician; also called order communications. See [Computerized physician order entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computerized_physician_order_entry). See Chapters 4.6, 4.7.

**Content design** — Designing the words, structure and formats that help people complete a task, in plain language and accessible forms. See Chapter 3.1.

**Cloud computing** — Delivering computing services over the internet on a public or hybrid basis. See [Cloud computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_computing). See Chapter 4.0.

**Caldicott Guardian** — A senior person responsible for protecting the confidentiality of health and care information and enabling appropriate sharing. See [Caldicott Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caldicott_Guardian). See Chapter 1.7.

**Change management** — The structured approach to moving people and organizations from a current to a future state. See [Change management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_management). See Chapter 8.1.

**Clinical safety (DCB0129/DCB0160)** — Mandatory NHS standards for managing clinical risk in the manufacture (DCB0129) and deployment (DCB0160) of health IT, overseen by a Clinical Safety Officer. See Chapter 1.6.

**Continuous delivery / integration** — Engineering practices for releasing software frequently and safely. See [Continuous delivery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_delivery) and [Continuous integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_integration). See Chapter 3.5.

**Conway's law** — The observation that a system's design mirrors the communication structure of the organization that builds it. See [Conway's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_law). See Chapter 7.0.

**Cost-effectiveness analysis** — Comparing the cost of interventions against a common health outcome (often the QALY) to judge value for money. See [Cost-effectiveness analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis). See Chapter 6.1.

**Cost of delay** — The economic cost of not having something finished yet; the numerator in prioritization methods such as WSJF. See [Cost of delay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_of_delay). See Chapter 2.2.

**Cyber security** — Protecting systems, networks and data from digital attack; in the NHS, guided by the NCSC and the Data Security and Protection Toolkit. See [Computer security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security). See Chapter 1.7.

**Cynefin** — A sense-making framework distinguishing clear, complicated, complex and chaotic contexts to match the response to the situation. See [Cynefin framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynefin_framework). See Chapters 1.3, 2.1.

**Concentration risk** — The exposure created by depending too heavily on a single supplier, platform or region. See [Concentration risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration_risk). See Chapter 6.6.

## D

**Digital public infrastructure** — Shared, reusable digital platforms and services (identity, data exchange, national APIs) that many organizations build on. See Chapter 4.1.

**Design system** — A single source of truth of reusable, accessible interface components, patterns and guidance (e.g. the NHS and GOV.UK design systems). See Chapter 3.2.

**Digital social care record (DSCR)** — A digital replacement for paper care records in adult social care, supplied from an assured list. See Chapter 9.3.

**DevOps** — A culture and set of practices uniting development and operations to release software frequently and safely. See [DevOps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DevOps). See Chapter 3.6.

**Digital literacy** — The capability of the whole workforce (not just specialists) to use digital tools confidently and safely. See Chapter 7.4.

**Data cleansing** — Detecting and correcting or removing inaccurate, corrupt or duplicate records. See [Data cleansing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_cleansing). See Chapter 4.9.

**Data ethics** — Doing the right thing with data beyond legal compliance; social licence and public trust. See [Big data ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_data_ethics). See Chapter 1.8.

**Data migration** — The one-off transfer of historical records from a legacy system into a new one; technically routine but clinically high-risk. See [Data migration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_migration). See Chapter 4.6.

**Data profiling** — Examining existing data to measure its actual condition before cleansing, migration or reporting. See [Data profiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_profiling). See Chapter 4.9.

**Data quality** — Fitness of data for use, judged across dimensions such as accuracy, completeness, timeliness, validity, consistency and uniqueness; in care, poor quality is a clinical-safety hazard. See [Data quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_quality). See Chapter 4.9.

**Data Quality Maturity Index (DQMI)** — NHS England's measure of the quality of the data organizations submit in national datasets. See Chapter 4.9.

**Data steward** — The named role accountable for the quality and fitness of a defined data domain. See [Data steward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_steward). See Chapter 4.9.

**Digital strategy** — A statement of digital vision, priorities and roadmap aligned to organizational and clinical strategy. See Chapter 1.2.

**Disaster recovery** — The technical recovery of systems and data after a major failure, measured by RTO and RPO. See [IT disaster recovery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IT_disaster_recovery). See Chapter 3.9.

**Dictionary of medicines and devices (dm+d)** — The NHS standard terminology giving every medicine and device a unique code for unambiguous recording and interoperability. See Chapter 4.8.

**Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT)** — The annual self-assessment NHS and social-care organizations complete to show they meet data-security and information-governance standards. See Chapter 1.7.

**Demand management** — Capturing, understanding and shaping the flow of incoming requests so supply can meet it. See Chapters 2.0, 5.3.

**Diffusion of innovations** — Rogers' theory of how new ideas and technology spread through a population over time. See [Diffusion of innovations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_of_innovations). See Chapter 8.0.

**Digital inclusion / exclusion** — Whether people can access and use digital services; exclusion (the [digital divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_divide)) maps closely onto health inequalities. See Chapters 1.9, 3.11.

**Discovery** — The first lifecycle phase: understanding users and the problem before committing to a solution. See Chapter 3.4.

**DORA metrics** — Four key measures of software delivery performance (deployment frequency, lead time, change-fail rate, time to restore). See [DevOps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DevOps). See Chapters 3.8, 10.0.

**Double Diamond** — The Design Council's model of divergent-then-convergent thinking across "discover/define" and "develop/deliver". See [Double Diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Diamond_(design_process_model)). See Chapters 3.0, 3.4.

**DTAC** — The NHS Digital Technology Assessment Criteria: a baseline for clinical safety, data protection, security, interoperability and usability of digital health products. See Chapters 1.6, 8.4.

**Data re-identification** — Matching supposedly anonymous data against other datasets to reveal who someone is; genomic data is inherently re-identifiable. See [Data re-identification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_re-identification). See Chapters 4.10, 4.11.

**Dynamic consent** — A digital, ongoing consent model letting people see and change how their data or samples are used over time. See [Dynamic consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_consent). See Chapter 4.11.

## E

**Externality** — A cost or benefit of a treatment falling on people not party to it, such as herd immunity from vaccination. See [Externality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Externality). See Chapter 6.1.

**E-iatrogenesis** — Patient harm caused or enabled by health IT, such as a pick-list or default-dose error. See [Iatrogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iatrogenesis). See Chapter 4.8.

**Electronic patient record (EPR)** — A provider's core clinical system: the digital patient record plus the tools clinicians use to order, document and review at the point of care (also EHR/EMR). See [Electronic health record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_health_record). See Chapter 4.6.

**Electronic prescribing & medicines administration (ePMA)** — A hospital system, usually an EPR module, managing the whole inpatient medicines process from prescribing to recorded administration. See [Electronic prescribing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_prescribing). See Chapter 4.8.

**Electronic Prescription Service (EPS)** — The NHS England service that sends prescriptions electronically from prescriber to pharmacy. See [NHS Electronic Prescription Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NHS_Electronic_Prescription_Service). See Chapter 4.8.

**Enterprise architecture** — A disciplined view of an organization's business, data, application and technology layers to guide change. See [Enterprise architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enterprise_architecture). See Chapter 4.0.

**Equality, Diversity & Inclusion (EDI)** — Fair treatment, representation and belonging in the workforce, underpinned by the Equality Act 2010 and duties such as WRES and WDES. See Chapter 7.6.

**Enterprise Project Portfolio Management (EPPM)** — Managing the entire portfolio of change initiatives as one coordinated whole. See [Project portfolio management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_portfolio_management). See Chapter 5.3.

**Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)** — Integrated software for finance, HR, payroll, procurement and supply chain that acts as an organization's system of record. See [Enterprise resource planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enterprise_resource_planning). See Chapter 5.4.

**Evidence hierarchy** — A ranking of evidence types by susceptibility to bias, from expert opinion up to systematic reviews of randomized trials. See [Hierarchy of evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy_of_evidence). See Chapter 1.3.

**Experience-level agreement (XLA)** — A commitment measuring the outcome a user actually experiences, complementing component-level SLAs. See Chapter 6.6.

## F

**FHIR** — Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources, the HL7 standard for exchanging health data. See [Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_Healthcare_Interoperability_Resources). See Chapter 4.3.

**FinOps (cloud financial operations)** — An operating discipline uniting engineering, finance and business/clinical teams to manage cloud cost as a shared, data-driven responsibility across an Inform/Optimize/Operate lifecycle. See Chapter 6.5.

**Five Case Model** — The HM Treasury / UK government standard structure for a business case. See Chapters 6.0, 9.1.

**Five rights of CDS** — The quality frame for decision support: the right information, to the right person, in the right format, through the right channel, at the right time. See Chapter 4.7.

**Frontline digitization** — NHS England's national programme setting a core electronic patient record as the baseline for every provider and tying capital funding to digital maturity. See Chapter 4.6.

**Federated analytics / federated learning** — Running queries or training models across separate data holdings so insight is combined without the raw data being pooled. See [Federated learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federated_learning). See Chapter 4.10.

**Five Safes** — A framework structuring safe data access across safe people, projects, settings, data and outputs. See [Five Safes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_safes). See Chapter 4.10.

## G

**GDPR** — The [General Data Protection Regulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Data_Protection_Regulation) (and UK GDPR): the legal framework for processing personal data. See Chapter 1.7.

**Golden record** — A single reconciled, trusted version of an entity (such as a patient) assembled from multiple source systems. See Chapter 4.9.

**Goodhart's law** — "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." See [Goodhart's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law). See Chapter 10.0.

**Greener NHS / net zero** — The NHS programme and commitment to reach net-zero carbon emissions across its own footprint and wider supply chain. See Chapter 8.5.

**Green Book** — HM Treasury guidance on appraising and evaluating public spending. See Chapters 6.0, 6.1, 9.1.

**GA4GH (Global Alliance for Genomics and Health)** — The international standards body for responsible genomic data sharing. See [Global Alliance for Genomics and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Alliance_for_Genomics_and_Health). See Chapter 4.11.

**Genomics** — The study of the whole genome and its use in care; genomic data is large, lifelong, familial and uniquely identifying. See [Genomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genomics). See Chapter 4.11.

**Genomics England / 100,000 Genomes Project** — The government-owned company and programme that sequenced 100,000 genomes and curates the National Genomic Research Library. See [Genomics England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genomics_England). See Chapter 4.11.

## H

**Hazard ratio (HR)** — The ratio of the event rate in a treatment group to a control group over time, from survival analysis; often misread because it averages over follow-up and does not translate simply into absolute risk or life-years. See [Hazard ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hazard_ratio). See Chapter 1.4.

**Health literacy** — People's ability to find, understand and use health information and services; low health literacy widens inequalities. See [Health literacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_literacy). See Chapter 3.1.

**Health Research Authority (HRA)** — The body overseeing research ethics and governance for health and social care research in the UK. See Chapter 8.6.

**Hazard log** — The living register of clinical hazards, their risk ratings and controls that sits at the heart of a clinical safety case. See Chapter 1.6.

**Health economics** — The study of how to allocate scarce resources to maximize health, using tools such as cost-effectiveness analysis and the QALY. See [Health economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_economics). See Chapter 6.1.

**Health inequalities** — Unfair and avoidable differences in health across the population; digital exclusion can widen them. See [Health equity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_equity). See Chapter 1.9.

**HIMSS EMRAM** — The HIMSS Electronic Medical Record Adoption Model, a 0–7 international benchmark of a provider's clinical-system digital maturity. See [Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthcare_Information_and_Management_Systems_Society). See Chapter 4.6.

**Hospital-at-home / virtual ward** — Delivering hospital-level acute care to patients in their own homes with daily clinical review and rapid escalation. See [Hospital-at-home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital-at-home). See Chapter 3.11.

## I

**Information asymmetry** — The imbalance where clinicians hold far more information about treatment than patients, one reason the healthcare market cannot be left to itself. See [Information asymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_asymmetry). See Chapter 6.1.

**Identity & access management (IAM)** — Controlling who can access what: authentication, authorization, role-based access control and least privilege. See Chapter 4.2.

**Internet of Medical Things (IoMT)** — Connected medical devices and sensors that generate and exchange data; a major clinical and security surface. See Chapter 4.4.

**ICER (incremental cost-effectiveness ratio)** — The extra cost per extra unit of health (e.g. per QALY) of one option versus another; compared against a threshold to judge value. See Chapter 6.1.

**Incident management** — The service-management practice of restoring normal service as quickly as possible after disruption, with major-incident and business-continuity procedures for the most serious. See [Incident management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incident_management). See Chapter 3.8.

**Information governance** — The rules and accountability for handling information legally, securely and ethically. See Chapter 1.7.

**Integrated Care System (ICS)** — A partnership of NHS and local-government organizations planning and delivering joined-up health and care in an area of England. See [Integrated care system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_care_system). See Chapters 5.2, 9.0.

**Interoperability** — The ability of systems to exchange and use information, at technical and semantic levels. See [Interoperability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interoperability). See Chapter 4.3.

**ITIL** — A framework for IT service management, including incident, problem, change and service-request management. See [ITIL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITIL). See Chapter 3.8.

**Incidental (secondary) findings** — Clinically significant discoveries unrelated to the reason a genomic test was ordered, raising consent and feedback questions. See [Incidental medical findings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incidental_medical_findings). See Chapter 4.11.

## K

**Kanban** — A pull-based method for visualizing and limiting work in progress to improve flow. See [Kanban (development)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanban_(development)). See Chapters 2.1, 2.2.

**Knowledge management** — The discipline of capturing, sharing and reusing an organization's knowledge, both tacit and explicit. See [Knowledge management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_management). See Chapter 10.3.

**KPI** — Key Performance Indicator: a metric tracking progress toward an objective. See [Performance indicator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Performance_indicator). See Chapter 10.0.

**Kotter's 8 steps** — John Kotter's model for leading organizational change, from creating urgency to anchoring change in the culture. See Chapter 8.1.

**Kraljic matrix** — A supplier and purchasing segmentation model classifying items by business criticality and supply-market difficulty. See [Kraljic matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kraljic_matrix). See Chapters 6.6, 6.3.

## L

**Legacy system** — Ageing hardware or software still in use, often unsupported — a safety, security and cost risk. See [Legacy system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legacy_system). See Chapter 4.0.

**Lean** — A philosophy of maximizing value and eliminating waste, originating in the Toyota Production System. See [Lean manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lean_manufacturing). See Chapters 2.0, 2.2.

**Live** — The lifecycle phase where a service runs in production and is continuously improved and eventually retired. See Chapters 3.5, 3.8.

**Logic model** — A diagram linking inputs and activities to outputs, outcomes and impact, used to plan and evaluate. See [Logic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_model). See Chapters 1.3, 6.1.

## M

**Model for Improvement** — A quality-improvement method pairing three questions with Plan-Do-Study-Act cycles. See Chapter 10.2.

**Machine learning** — A branch of AI in which systems learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. See [Machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning). See Chapter 8.4.

**Master data management** — Governing an organization's core shared data about key entities — patients, staff, organizations, medicines, suppliers — so it is consistent and authoritative across systems. See [Master data management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_data_management). See Chapters 4.9, 5.4.

**Maturity model** — A staged description of capability from initial to optimizing, used for self-assessment. See [Capability Maturity Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model). Used in every chapter.

**Medical algorithm** — A computation, formula, score or look-up used in care; the basis of clinical calculators and rule-based decision support. See [Medical algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_algorithm). See Chapter 4.7.

**Medicines reconciliation** — Building an accurate list of a patient's current medicines and comparing it against what is prescribed at every transfer of care. See [Medication reconciliation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication_reconciliation). See Chapter 4.8.

**mHealth** — Health services and information delivered through mobile devices and apps. See [mHealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHealth). See Chapter 3.11.

**MHRA** — The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency, which regulates medical devices including software and AI as a medical device. See [Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicines_and_Healthcare_products_Regulatory_Agency). See Chapters 1.6, 8.4.

**Minimum viable product (MVP)** — The smallest version of a product that delivers and tests value. See [Minimum viable product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_viable_product). See Chapters 5.0, 8.0.

**MLOps** — The practices for deploying, monitoring and maintaining machine-learning models in production, including watching for model drift. See Chapter 8.4.

**MoSCoW** — A prioritization method: Must, Should, Could, Won't have. See [MoSCoW method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MoSCoW_method). See Chapter 2.2.

**MSP (Managing Successful Programmes)** — AXELOS's programme-management framework. See Chapter 5.2.

**Multidisciplinary team** — A team combining the different skills needed to deliver a whole service. See Chapters 1.0, 7.0.

## N

**Nudge theory** — Shaping the choice environment to make a helpful behaviour easier without removing options. See [Nudge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nudge_theory). See Chapter 8.3.

**NHS App** — The national patient-facing app (with NHS login) for access to records and services. See [NHS App](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NHS_app). See Chapter 3.3.

**NHS number** — The unique 10-digit identifier for every patient in England and Wales; correct use is the foundation of safe record matching. See Chapter 4.9.

**Net zero** — Balancing the greenhouse gases emitted with those removed; the NHS has committed to net zero across its carbon footprint. See [Net zero emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_zero_emissions). See Chapter 8.5.

**NICE Evidence Standards Framework** — NICE's framework for the evidence digital health technologies should show for their effectiveness and economic impact. See Chapters 6.1, 8.4.

## O

**OKR** — Objectives and Key Results: a goal-setting framework pairing an ambition with measurable results. See [OKR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OKR). See Chapter 10.0.

**Operating model** — How an organization is arranged to deliver value: value streams, capabilities, teams, governance, technology, data and suppliers. See [Operating model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_model). See Chapter 1.1.

**Order set** — A bundled group of orders (tests, drugs, instructions) appropriate to a clinical situation, presented together to speed and standardize care. See Chapter 4.7.

**Organizational development (OD)** — Planned effort to improve an organization's health, capability and culture. See [Organization development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_development). See Chapter 7.5.

**Outcomes vs outputs** — Outputs are the activity a service produces (e.g. appointments delivered); outcomes are the change in health and wellbeing that results. Measuring both consistently is central to demonstrating value. See Chapters 6.1, 10.0.

## P

**Platform (as a product)** — A shared capability run as a product with internal users, so teams build on it rather than rebuilding it. See Chapter 4.1.

**Patient administration system (PAS)** — The system managing admissions, discharges, transfers and outpatient appointments; historically the administrative core of a hospital. See [Hospital information system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital_information_system). See Chapter 4.6.

**Prototyping** — Making low- or high-fidelity versions of a service to test ideas with users before building. See [Software prototyping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_prototyping). See Chapter 3.2.

**PDSA** — Plan-Do-Study-Act: the small-test-of-change cycle at the heart of quality improvement. See Chapter 10.2.

**Personal health record (PHR)** — A record of health information controlled or accessed by the individual. See [Personal health record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_health_record). See Chapter 3.3.

**Polypharmacy** — The simultaneous use of multiple medicines, common in older and multi-morbid patients and a driver of harm and interactions. See [Polypharmacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polypharmacy). See Chapter 4.8.

**Population health management** — Using data to segment and stratify a population and target interventions to those who benefit most. See Chapter 4.5.

**Pilotitis** — The anti-pattern of endless small pilots that never scale. See Chapter 8.0.

**PRINCE2** — A structured project-management method widely used in UK government. See [PRINCE2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PRINCE2). See Chapter 5.1.

**Procurement (public)** — Buying goods and services under public-procurement rules (in the UK, the Procurement Act 2023), via routes such as framework agreements. See [Government procurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_procurement). See Chapter 6.3.

**Product management** — The discipline of leading a durable product team to deliver outcomes for users and the organization. See [Product management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_management). See Chapter 5.0.

**Productivity dip** — The planned, temporary fall in clinical throughput that typically follows an EPR go-live as staff learn the new system. See Chapter 4.6.

**Programme management** — Coordinating related projects and products to deliver strategic outcomes and benefits. See [Program management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_management). See Chapter 5.2.

**PROMs / PREMs** — Patient-Reported Outcome Measures and Patient-Reported Experience Measures: standardized ways to capture outcomes and experience directly from patients. See Chapter 6.1.

**Psychological safety** — A shared belief that it is safe to speak up, admit mistakes and take interpersonal risks. See [Psychological safety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_safety). See Chapter 7.5.

**Pharmacogenomics** — Using a person's genome to predict which medicines will work or cause harm, ideally surfaced as an alert at the point of prescribing. See [Pharmacogenomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmacogenomics). See Chapters 4.11, 4.8.

**Precision (personalized) medicine** — Tailoring prevention and treatment to an individual's genome, characteristics and circumstances. See [Precision medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precision_medicine). See Chapter 4.11.

**Pseudonymization** — Replacing direct identifiers with key-linked tokens; the data remains personal data in law, unlike true anonymization. See [Pseudonymization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudonymization). See Chapters 4.10, 1.7.

## Q

**Quality improvement (QI)** — Frontline-led, iterative improvement using methods such as the Model for Improvement and PDSA. See Chapter 10.2.

**QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year)** — A measure combining length and quality of life into a single unit, used to compare the health value of different interventions. See [Quality-adjusted life year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year). See Chapter 6.1.

## R

**Retrospective** — A regular team review that turns what happened into concrete improvements to how the team works. See Chapter 7.1.

**Regulation & compliance** — The terrain of statutory regulators, mandatory standards and voluntary assurance that digital health must satisfy. See Chapter 1.5.

**Record linkage** — Matching records across different sources that refer to the same real-world entity; the basis of patient matching. See [Record linkage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Record_linkage). See Chapter 4.9.

**Reference data** — Data used to classify or categorize other data (code lists, value sets, organization codes), usually externally defined and slow to change. See [Reference data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_data). See Chapter 4.9.

**Remote patient monitoring (RPM)** — Streaming of physiological readings from a patient's home to a clinical team who review and act on them. See [Remote patient monitoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_patient_monitoring). See Chapters 3.11, 4.4.

**Role-based access control (RBAC)** — Granting access by job role rather than to individuals, supporting least privilege. See Chapter 4.2.

**Real-world evidence** — Evidence on how an intervention performs in routine practice, not only in trials. See Chapter 8.6.

**RTO / RPO** — Recovery Time Objective and Recovery Point Objective: how fast, and to what point, a service must recover. See Chapter 3.9.

**RACI** — A responsibility-assignment matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed). See [Responsibility assignment matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Responsibility_assignment_matrix). See Chapters 1.1, 7.0.

**RAID log** — A register of Risks, Assumptions, Issues and Dependencies. See Chapters 5.1, 6.4.

**Randomized controlled trial (RCT)** — An experiment that randomly allocates participants to test an intervention's effect. See [Randomized controlled trial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial). See Chapter 1.3.

**Records management** — Governing the creation, retention and disposal of records, including statutory retention of health records. See [Records management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_management). See Chapters 1.7, 10.3.

**RICE** — A prioritization score: Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. See Chapter 2.2.

**Reference genome** — A standardized genome assembly against which an individual's DNA is compared; its version affects the interpretation. See [Reference genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_genome). See Chapter 4.11.

**Retained organization** — In a multi-sourced/SIAM model, the customer-side functions (strategy, demand, architecture, commercial, duty of care) that cannot be outsourced. See Chapter 6.6.

## S

**Shared services** — Common capabilities provided once and used by many teams or organizations, reducing duplication and cost. See Chapter 4.1.

**Safeguarding** — Protecting children and adults at risk from abuse, neglect and harm, including risks created by digital services. See [Safeguarding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safeguarding). See Chapter 1.10.

**Secure Data Environment (SDE)** — A trusted platform where approved analysts work with approved data for approved purposes without extracting it; also called a Trusted Research Environment (TRE). See Chapters 4.10, 4.5.

**Section 251 / Confidentiality Advisory Group (CAG)** — The route for lawfully using confidential patient information without consent, on independent advice, in England and Wales. See Chapter 4.10.

**Service integration and management (SIAM)** — An operating model coordinating multiple suppliers to deliver one end-to-end service through a single integrating layer. See [Service integration and management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_integration_and_management). See Chapter 6.6.

**Service integrator** — The SIAM layer (internal, external or hybrid) that coordinates providers and owns cross-cutting processes such as major-incident management. See Chapter 6.6.

**Statistical disclosure control** — Techniques such as suppressing small cell counts that stop published results from identifying individuals. See [Statistical disclosure control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_disclosure_control). See Chapter 4.10.

**Service evaluation** — Assessing how well a service works, distinct from formal research and clinical audit. See Chapter 8.6.

**Showback** — Reporting to each team what its technology consumption costs, for transparency, without moving money between budgets. See Chapter 6.5.

**Single source of truth** — Mastering each data element in exactly one authoritative place, so every other system refers back to it. See [Single source of truth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_source_of_truth). See Chapter 4.9.

**Software asset management (SAM)** — The discipline of tracking and optimizing owned and rented software, including SaaS and per-user licences. See [Software asset management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_asset_management). See Chapter 6.5.

**Stakeholder engagement** — Two-way involvement of the people affected by a change, beyond one-way communication. See [Stakeholder engagement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_engagement). See Chapter 8.2.

**SAFe** — The Scaled Agile Framework for coordinating many agile teams. See [Scaled agile framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaled_agile_framework). See Chapters 2.2, 5.2.

**SaMD (Software as a Medical Device)** — Software intended for a medical purpose that is itself a regulated medical device. See Chapters 1.6, 8.4.

**Scrum** — An agile framework using fixed-length sprints and defined roles. See [Scrum (software development)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)). See Chapter 3.5.

**Senior Responsible Owner (SRO)** — The single named individual accountable for a project or programme achieving its objectives. See Chapter 6.4.

**Service assessment** — A peer review against the NHS/GOV.UK Service Standard at lifecycle phase boundaries. See Chapters 1.0, 3.5.

**Service design** — Designing the whole end-to-end service across channels and touchpoints, not just the digital interface. See [Service design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_design). See Chapter 3.0.

**Service level agreement (SLA/SLO)** — A committed level of service (agreement) and the internal objective that supports it (objective); paired with error budgets in SRE. See Chapter 3.8.

**Service standard** — The set of principles a good public digital service must meet. See Chapters 1.0, 3.5.

**Site reliability engineering (SRE)** — An operations discipline applying software engineering to reliability, using SLOs and error budgets. See [Site reliability engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Site_reliability_engineering). See Chapter 3.8.

**Six-step methodology** — A widely used NHS/social-care workforce-planning method (define the plan, the workforce, the future, the gaps, the actions, and monitor). See Chapter 7.2.

**SNOMED CT** — The comprehensive clinical terminology used across the NHS to record care in a coded, computable way. See [SNOMED CT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNOMED_CT). See Chapter 4.3.

**Social value** — The wider economic, social and environmental benefit a contract can deliver, which public bodies must weigh in procurement. See Chapters 6.3, 8.5.

**Statistical process control (SPC)** — Using control charts to tell normal variation from real change; the NHS "Making Data Count" approach. See [Statistical process control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_process_control). See Chapter 10.0.

**Stakeholder analysis** — Identifying and understanding the people affected by, or able to affect, a change. See [Stakeholder analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_analysis). See Chapter 8.1.

## T

**Technology-enabled care (TEC)** — Telecare, telehealth and connected devices that help people live independently; widely used in social care. See Chapter 9.3.

**Telecare** — Sensors and alarms that help people stay safe and independent at home; widely used in adult social care. See [Telecare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecare). See Chapters 9.3, 3.11.

**Telehealth** — Using telecommunications technology to deliver care at a distance — consultation, monitoring, education and administration. See [Telehealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telehealth). See Chapter 3.11.

**Test automation** — Automated checks that run as part of continuous integration to catch regressions early. See [Test automation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_automation). See Chapter 3.7.

**Target operating model (TOM)** — The desired future-state operating model an organization is designing toward. See Chapter 1.1.

**Team Topologies** — A model (from the book by Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais) of four team types (stream-aligned, platform, enabling, complicated-subsystem) and three interaction modes; see also [Conway's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_law). See Chapter 7.0.

**Technical debt** — The future cost of expedient short-term technical choices. See [Technical debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_debt). See Chapter 2.2.

**Thinnest viable platform** — The smallest platform that reduces teams' cognitive load without becoming a bottleneck. See Chapter 1.1.

**Three lines of defence** — A risk-and-assurance model separating operational management, oversight functions, and independent internal audit. See Chapter 6.4.

**Total cost of ownership (TCO)** — The full direct and indirect cost of a technology over its whole life, including support, integration and exit. See [Total cost of ownership](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_cost_of_ownership). See Chapter 6.5.

**Total triage** — An access model where every request for care is assessed first and directed to the most appropriate response, rather than defaulting to face-to-face. See Chapter 3.11.

**Triage** — Assessing and routing incoming work by urgency and importance; borrowed from clinical practice. See [Triage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triage). See Chapter 2.1.

**Third-party management** — Overseeing external providers and their sub-contractors (fourth parties) across the supply chain. See [Third-party management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third-party_management). See Chapter 6.6.

## U

**Unit economics** — Expressing cost as a rate against a meaningful unit — cost per patient, appointment or API call — to weigh spend against value. See Chapter 6.5.

**User research** — Studying users' needs and behaviours to inform design decisions. See [User research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_research). See Chapter 3.0.

## V

**Value management** — Tracking and banking the benefits and value a change was meant to deliver, distinct from appraising it up front. See Chapter 6.2.

**Vendor lock-in** — Dependence on a supplier that makes switching costly, often through proprietary data or interfaces; open standards and exit planning reduce it. See [Vendor lock-in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor_lock-in). See Chapters 4.3, 6.3, 6.6.

**Virtual ward (hospital-at-home)** — Delivering hospital-level acute care to patients in their own homes, with daily clinical review, monitoring and rapid escalation. See [Hospital-at-home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital-at-home). See Chapter 3.11.

## W

**WHO digital health strategy** — The World Health Organization's global strategy framing digital health and digital public goods across health systems. See Chapter 9.2.

**What Good Looks Like (WGLL)** — The NHS framework describing success measures for digital maturity. See Chapter 1.2.

**Waterfall** — A sequential, plan-driven delivery approach. See [Waterfall model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterfall_model). See Chapters 3.5, 5.1.

**Watermelon reporting** — Status that looks green on the outside but is red inside; a reporting anti-pattern. See Chapter 10.4.

**Workforce planning** — Matching the supply of people and skills to future demand. See [Workforce planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workforce_planning). See Chapter 7.2.

**Workforce strategy** — The long-term, evidence-based plan for the shape, capability, culture and wellbeing of the workforce. See Chapter 7.3.

**WSJF (Weighted Shortest Job First)** — A prioritization method dividing cost of delay by job size. See Chapter 2.2.


**Whole genome sequencing (WGS)** — Reading effectively an individual's entire genome; the most data-intensive sequencing option. See [Whole genome sequencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whole_genome_sequencing). See Chapter 4.11.

## Z

**Zero trust** — A security model that trusts no request by default and verifies every access continuously. See Chapter 4.2.
