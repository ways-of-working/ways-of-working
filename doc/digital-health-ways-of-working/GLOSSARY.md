# Glossary

Definitions of the key terms used throughout *Ways of Working for Digital Health & Care*. Where a term has a good encyclopaedic entry, a Wikipedia link is provided for further reading. Cross-references point to the chapter where the term is developed most fully.

## A

**Accessibility** — Designing services so people with disabilities can use them; in UK public services, measured against the [Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_Content_Accessibility_Guidelines) and a legal duty. See Chapters 6, 10.

**ADKAR** — Prosci's individual change model: Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. A people-centred complement to organisational change models. See Chapter 29.

**Agile** — A family of iterative, incremental ways of working that deliver value in small increments and adapt to feedback. See [Agile software development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agile_software_development). See Chapters 1, 12.

**Alpha** — The lifecycle phase where a team tests solutions to the problems found in discovery, usually with throwaway prototypes. See Chapter 12.

**Architecture decision record (ADR)** — A short document capturing one significant design decision, its context and its consequences, kept close to the code. See Chapter 35.

**Artificial intelligence (AI)** — Computer systems performing tasks that normally require human intelligence; in health, increasingly delivered through machine learning. See [Artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_intelligence). See Chapter 30.

**Assisted digital** — Support for people who cannot use a digital service independently, so they are not excluded. See Chapter 6.

## B

**Balanced scorecard** — A strategic performance framework balancing financial, customer, internal-process, and learning-and-growth measures. See [Balanced scorecard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balanced_scorecard). See Chapter 34.

**Benefits realisation** — The discipline of identifying, planning, measuring and actually achieving the benefits a change is meant to deliver. See [Benefits realisation management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benefits_realisation_management). See Chapters 17, 18, 29.

**Beta** — The lifecycle phase where a team builds and iterates a working service with real users, first privately then publicly. See Chapter 12.

**Bus factor** — The number of people who would have to be lost before a system or service could no longer be run or understood; a measure of key-person risk. See [Bus factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_factor). See Chapter 35.

**Business case** — The justification for an investment, in the UK public sector usually the [Five Case Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_case) (strategic, economic, commercial, financial, management). See Chapters 20, 33.

## C

**Caldicott Guardian** — A senior person responsible for protecting the confidentiality of health and care information and enabling appropriate sharing. See [Caldicott Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caldicott_Guardian). See Chapter 5.

**Change management** — The structured approach to moving people and organisations from a current to a future state. See [Change management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_management). See Chapter 29.

**Clinical safety (DCB0129/DCB0160)** — Mandatory NHS standards for managing clinical risk in the manufacture (DCB0129) and deployment (DCB0160) of health IT, overseen by a Clinical Safety Officer. See Chapter 4.

**Continuous delivery / integration** — Engineering practices for releasing software frequently and safely. See [Continuous delivery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_delivery) and [Continuous integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_integration). See Chapter 12.

**Conway's law** — The observation that a system's design mirrors the communication structure of the organisation that builds it. See [Conway's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_law). See Chapter 24.

**Cost-effectiveness analysis** — Comparing the cost of interventions against a common health outcome (often the QALY) to judge value for money. See [Cost-effectiveness analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost-effectiveness_analysis). See Chapter 21.

**Cost of delay** — The economic cost of not having something finished yet; the numerator in prioritisation methods such as WSJF. See [Cost of delay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_of_delay). See Chapter 9.

**Cyber security** — Protecting systems, networks and data from digital attack; in the NHS, guided by the NCSC and the Data Security and Protection Toolkit. See [Computer security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security). See Chapter 5.

**Cynefin** — A sense-making framework distinguishing clear, complicated, complex and chaotic contexts to match the response to the situation. See [Cynefin framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynefin_framework). See Chapters 3, 8.

## D

**Data Security and Protection Toolkit (DSPT)** — The annual self-assessment NHS and social-care organisations complete to show they meet data-security and information-governance standards. See Chapter 5.

**Demand management** — Capturing, understanding and shaping the flow of incoming requests so supply can meet it. See Chapters 7, 18.

**Diffusion of innovations** — Rogers' theory of how new ideas and technology spread through a population over time. See [Diffusion of innovations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_of_innovations). See Chapter 28.

**Digital inclusion / exclusion** — Whether people can access and use digital services; exclusion (the [digital divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_divide)) maps closely onto health inequalities. See Chapter 6.

**Discovery** — The first lifecycle phase: understanding users and the problem before committing to a solution. See Chapter 11.

**DORA metrics** — Four key measures of software delivery performance (deployment frequency, lead time, change-fail rate, time to restore). See [DevOps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DevOps). See Chapters 14, 34.

**Double Diamond** — The Design Council's model of divergent-then-convergent thinking across "discover/define" and "develop/deliver". See [Double Diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Diamond_(design_process_model)). See Chapters 10, 11.

**DTAC** — The NHS Digital Technology Assessment Criteria: a baseline for clinical safety, data protection, security, interoperability and usability of digital health products. See Chapters 4, 30.

## E

**Enterprise Project Portfolio Management (EPPM)** — Managing the entire portfolio of change initiatives as one coordinated whole. See [Project portfolio management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_portfolio_management). See Chapter 18.

**Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)** — Integrated software for finance, HR, payroll, procurement and supply chain that acts as an organisation's system of record. See [Enterprise resource planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enterprise_resource_planning). See Chapter 19.

**Evidence hierarchy** — A ranking of evidence types by susceptibility to bias, from expert opinion up to systematic reviews of randomised trials. See [Hierarchy of evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierarchy_of_evidence). See Chapter 3.

## F

**FHIR** — Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources, the HL7 standard for exchanging health data. See [Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_Healthcare_Interoperability_Resources). See Chapter 13.

**Five Case Model** — The HM Treasury / UK government standard structure for a business case. See Chapters 20, 33.

## G

**GDPR** — The [General Data Protection Regulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Data_Protection_Regulation) (and UK GDPR): the legal framework for processing personal data. See Chapter 5.

**Goodhart's law** — "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." See [Goodhart's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_law). See Chapter 34.

**Greener NHS / net zero** — The NHS programme and commitment to reach net-zero carbon emissions across its own footprint and wider supply chain. See Chapter 31.

**Green Book** — HM Treasury guidance on appraising and evaluating public spending. See Chapters 20, 21, 33.

## H

**Hazard log** — The living register of clinical hazards, their risk ratings and controls that sits at the heart of a clinical safety case. See Chapter 4.

**Health economics** — The study of how to allocate scarce resources to maximise health, using tools such as cost-effectiveness analysis and the QALY. See [Health economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_economics). See Chapter 21.

**Health inequalities** — Unfair and avoidable differences in health across the population; digital exclusion can widen them. See [Health equity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_equity). See Chapter 6.

## I

**ICER (incremental cost-effectiveness ratio)** — The extra cost per extra unit of health (e.g. per QALY) of one option versus another; compared against a threshold to judge value. See Chapter 21.

**Incident management** — The service-management practice of restoring normal service as quickly as possible after disruption, with major-incident and business-continuity procedures for the most serious. See [Incident management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incident_management). See Chapter 14.

**Information governance** — The rules and accountability for handling information legally, securely and ethically. See Chapter 5.

**Integrated Care System (ICS)** — A partnership of NHS and local-government organisations planning and delivering joined-up health and care in an area of England. See [Integrated care system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_care_system). See Chapters 17, 32.

**Interoperability** — The ability of systems to exchange and use information, at technical and semantic levels. See [Interoperability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interoperability). See Chapter 13.

**ITIL** — A framework for IT service management, including incident, problem, change and service-request management. See [ITIL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITIL). See Chapter 14.

## K

**Kanban** — A pull-based method for visualising and limiting work in progress to improve flow. See [Kanban (development)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kanban_(development)). See Chapters 8, 9.

**Knowledge management** — The discipline of capturing, sharing and reusing an organisation's knowledge, both tacit and explicit. See [Knowledge management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_management). See Chapter 35.

**KPI** — Key Performance Indicator: a metric tracking progress toward an objective. See [Performance indicator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Performance_indicator). See Chapter 34.

**Kotter's 8 steps** — John Kotter's model for leading organisational change, from creating urgency to anchoring change in the culture. See Chapter 29.

## L

**Lean** — A philosophy of maximising value and eliminating waste, originating in the Toyota Production System. See [Lean manufacturing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lean_manufacturing). See Chapters 7, 9.

**Live** — The lifecycle phase where a service runs in production and is continuously improved and eventually retired. See Chapters 12, 14.

**Logic model** — A diagram linking inputs and activities to outputs, outcomes and impact, used to plan and evaluate. See [Logic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_model). See Chapters 3, 21.

## M

**Machine learning** — A branch of AI in which systems learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. See [Machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning). See Chapter 30.

**Master data management** — Governing an organisation's core shared reference data (patients, staff, suppliers) for consistency. See [Master data management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_data_management). See Chapter 19.

**Maturity model** — A staged description of capability from initial to optimising, used for self-assessment. See [Capability Maturity Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capability_Maturity_Model). Used in every chapter.

**MHRA** — The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency, which regulates medical devices including software and AI as a medical device. See [Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicines_and_Healthcare_products_Regulatory_Agency). See Chapters 4, 30.

**Minimum viable product (MVP)** — The smallest version of a product that delivers and tests value. See [Minimum viable product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_viable_product). See Chapters 15, 28.

**MLOps** — The practices for deploying, monitoring and maintaining machine-learning models in production, including watching for model drift. See Chapter 30.

**MoSCoW** — A prioritisation method: Must, Should, Could, Won't have. See [MoSCoW method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MoSCoW_method). See Chapter 9.

**MSP (Managing Successful Programmes)** — AXELOS's programme-management framework. See Chapter 17.

**Multidisciplinary team** — A team combining the different skills needed to deliver a whole service. See Chapters 1, 24.

## N

**Net zero** — Balancing the greenhouse gases emitted with those removed; the NHS has committed to net zero across its carbon footprint. See [Net zero emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_zero_emissions). See Chapter 31.

**NICE Evidence Standards Framework** — NICE's framework for the evidence digital health technologies should show for their effectiveness and economic impact. See Chapters 21, 30.

## O

**OKR** — Objectives and Key Results: a goal-setting framework pairing an ambition with measurable results. See [OKR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OKR). See Chapter 34.

**Operating model** — How an organisation is arranged to deliver value: value streams, capabilities, teams, governance, technology, data and suppliers. See [Operating model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operating_model). See Chapter 2.

**Organisational development (OD)** — Planned effort to improve an organisation's health, capability and culture. See [Organization development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_development). See Chapter 27.

**Outcomes vs outputs** — Outputs are the activity a service produces (e.g. appointments delivered); outcomes are the change in health and wellbeing that results. Measuring both consistently is central to demonstrating value. See Chapters 21, 34.

## P

**Pilotitis** — The anti-pattern of endless small pilots that never scale. See Chapter 28.

**PRINCE2** — A structured project-management method widely used in UK government. See [PRINCE2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PRINCE2). See Chapter 16.

**Procurement (public)** — Buying goods and services under public-procurement rules (in the UK, the Procurement Act 2023), via routes such as framework agreements. See [Government procurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_procurement). See Chapter 22.

**Product management** — The discipline of leading a durable product team to deliver outcomes for users and the organisation. See [Product management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_management). See Chapter 15.

**Programme management** — Coordinating related projects and products to deliver strategic outcomes and benefits. See [Program management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Program_management). See Chapter 17.

**PROMs / PREMs** — Patient-Reported Outcome Measures and Patient-Reported Experience Measures: standardised ways to capture outcomes and experience directly from patients. See Chapter 21.

**Psychological safety** — A shared belief that it is safe to speak up, admit mistakes and take interpersonal risks. See [Psychological safety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_safety). See Chapter 27.

## Q

**QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year)** — A measure combining length and quality of life into a single unit, used to compare the health value of different interventions. See [Quality-adjusted life year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year). See Chapter 21.

## R

**RACI** — A responsibility-assignment matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed). See [Responsibility assignment matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Responsibility_assignment_matrix). See Chapters 2, 24.

**RAID log** — A register of Risks, Assumptions, Issues and Dependencies. See Chapters 16, 23.

**Randomised controlled trial (RCT)** — An experiment that randomly allocates participants to test an intervention's effect. See [Randomized controlled trial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial). See Chapter 3.

**Records management** — Governing the creation, retention and disposal of records, including statutory retention of health records. See [Records management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_management). See Chapters 5, 35.

**RICE** — A prioritisation score: Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. See Chapter 9.

## S

**SAFe** — The Scaled Agile Framework for coordinating many agile teams. See [Scaled agile framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaled_agile_framework). See Chapters 9, 17.

**SaMD (Software as a Medical Device)** — Software intended for a medical purpose that is itself a regulated medical device. See Chapters 4, 30.

**Scrum** — An agile framework using fixed-length sprints and defined roles. See [Scrum (software development)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scrum_(software_development)). See Chapter 12.

**Senior Responsible Owner (SRO)** — The single named individual accountable for a project or programme achieving its objectives. See Chapter 23.

**Service assessment** — A peer review against the NHS/GOV.UK Service Standard at lifecycle phase boundaries. See Chapters 1, 12.

**Service design** — Designing the whole end-to-end service across channels and touchpoints, not just the digital interface. See [Service design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_design). See Chapter 10.

**Service level agreement (SLA/SLO)** — A committed level of service (agreement) and the internal objective that supports it (objective); paired with error budgets in SRE. See Chapter 14.

**Service standard** — The set of principles a good public digital service must meet. See Chapters 1, 12.

**Site reliability engineering (SRE)** — An operations discipline applying software engineering to reliability, using SLOs and error budgets. See [Site reliability engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Site_reliability_engineering). See Chapter 14.

**Six-step methodology** — A widely used NHS/social-care workforce-planning method (define the plan, the workforce, the future, the gaps, the actions, and monitor). See Chapter 25.

**SNOMED CT** — The comprehensive clinical terminology used across the NHS to record care in a coded, computable way. See [SNOMED CT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNOMED_CT). See Chapter 13.

**Social value** — The wider economic, social and environmental benefit a contract can deliver, which public bodies must weigh in procurement. See Chapters 22, 31.

**Statistical process control (SPC)** — Using control charts to tell normal variation from real change; the NHS "Making Data Count" approach. See [Statistical process control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_process_control). See Chapter 34.

**Stakeholder analysis** — Identifying and understanding the people affected by, or able to affect, a change. See [Stakeholder analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stakeholder_analysis). See Chapter 29.

## T

**Target operating model (TOM)** — The desired future-state operating model an organisation is designing toward. See Chapter 2.

**Team Topologies** — A model (from the book by Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais) of four team types (stream-aligned, platform, enabling, complicated-subsystem) and three interaction modes; see also [Conway's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conway%27s_law). See Chapter 24.

**Technical debt** — The future cost of expedient short-term technical choices. See [Technical debt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_debt). See Chapter 9.

**Thinnest viable platform** — The smallest platform that reduces teams' cognitive load without becoming a bottleneck. See Chapter 2.

**Three lines of defence** — A risk-and-assurance model separating operational management, oversight functions, and independent internal audit. See Chapter 23.

**Triage** — Assessing and routing incoming work by urgency and importance; borrowed from clinical practice. See [Triage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triage). See Chapter 8.

## U

**User research** — Studying users' needs and behaviours to inform design decisions. See [User research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_research). See Chapter 10.

## V

**Vendor lock-in** — Dependence on a supplier that makes switching costly, often through proprietary data or interfaces; open standards and exit planning reduce it. See [Vendor lock-in](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vendor_lock-in). See Chapters 13, 22.

## W

**Waterfall** — A sequential, plan-driven delivery approach. See [Waterfall model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waterfall_model). See Chapters 12, 16.

**Watermelon reporting** — Status that looks green on the outside but is red inside; a reporting anti-pattern. See Chapter 36.

**Workforce planning** — Matching the supply of people and skills to future demand. See [Workforce planning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workforce_planning). See Chapter 25.

**Workforce strategy** — The long-term, evidence-based plan for the shape, capability, culture and wellbeing of the workforce. See Chapter 26.

**WSJF (Weighted Shortest Job First)** — A prioritisation method dividing cost of delay by job size. See Chapter 9.
