# Chapter 3.11 — Telehealth, Remote Consultation & Virtual Wards

**Moving care out of the clinic and into the phone, the screen and the home changes not just where care happens but who is accountable for it, who can reach it, and how you know it is safe — so remote care is an operating-model decision, not a technology purchase.**

## Why this matters in health and care

Remote care used to be a fringe option; now it is a mainstream way of delivering health and social care. A patient with a long-term condition may have a video appointment with a specialist, wear a monitor that streams readings to a team miles away, and be treated for an acute illness in their own bedroom under a "virtual ward" instead of a hospital bed. Each of these is a genuine model of care, with its own clinical risks, staffing model and accountability trail — not merely a digital convenience bolted onto the old pathway.

The stakes are high in three directions at once. Safety is the first: a remote clinician sees less than one in the room — no smell of ketones, no handshake that reveals a tremor, no colleague glancing over — and a monitoring stream that no one is resourced to watch is a false reassurance dressed up as a service. Equity is the second: the people who most need care are often those least able to join a video call, own a smartphone, or trust a device in their living room, so a poorly designed remote service can quietly widen the very inequalities the NHS exists to close. Public trust is the third: putting cameras, sensors and data flows into someone's home crosses a threshold of intimacy that a waiting room never did, and consent, safeguarding and privacy have to be earned there, not assumed.

There is also a hard operational reason to get this right. Remote and virtual models are being asked to carry real capacity — to keep people out of hospital, to free beds, to reach rural and housebound patients — and they only deliver those benefits if the operating model behind them is sound. A virtual ward with the wrong staffing ratio, an unclear escalation route, or a triage front door that funnels the wrong patients to the wrong channel does not save capacity; it manufactures risk and rework. How you design the way of working *is* the clinical service.

## Core concepts

[Telehealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telehealth) is the use of electronic information and telecommunications technologies to support health care at a distance — clinical consultation, monitoring, education and administration. It is the umbrella term; "telemedicine" is often used more narrowly for remote clinical consultation specifically, and the two are frequently used interchangeably. Telehealth sits within the broader field of [eHealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EHealth) — health care supported by digital processes and communication — and overlaps with [mHealth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHealth), the delivery of health services through mobile devices and apps.

It helps to separate telehealth into three **modalities**, because each has a different risk and workflow profile. Synchronous consultation happens in real time by telephone or by [video call](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Videotelephony), with clinician and patient present together at a distance. Asynchronous or "store-and-forward" consultation — sometimes called e-consultation — exchanges information without both parties being present at once: a patient submits symptoms, photographs or a form, and a clinician responds later. [Remote patient monitoring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_patient_monitoring) (RPM) streams physiological readings — blood pressure, oxygen saturation, blood glucose, weight — from the patient's home to a clinical team who review and act on them. Most real services blend all three.

A **virtual ward** — internationally often called [hospital-at-home](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hospital-at-home) — delivers hospital-level acute care to patients in their own homes, supported by daily clinical review, remote monitoring and the ability to escalate rapidly. It is a genuine ward with a virtual footprint: a defined patient list, a named clinical lead, a multidisciplinary team, and admission and discharge criteria. NHS England has invested heavily in virtual wards to expand acute capacity, particularly for frailty and acute respiratory infection. The device fleet and clinical engineering that make monitoring possible are covered elsewhere (see Chapter 4.4 — Connected Devices, IoT & Clinical Engineering); this chapter owns the *care model* that sits on top of them.

Two access concepts complete the picture. **Total triage** is an operating model in which every request for care — however it arrives — is assessed first and directed to the most appropriate response, rather than defaulting to a face-to-face appointment. It is [triage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triage) applied to the whole front door, often described as "digital-first" access when an online form is the first step. Done well it matches need to channel; done badly it becomes a barrier that the digitally excluded cannot cross. That risk connects directly to [health equity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_equity) and the [digital divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_divide) — the unequal access to devices, connectivity, skills and confidence that determines who can actually use a remote service. And because care now enters the home, [informed consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informed_consent) — a person understanding what is being monitored, by whom, and to what end — becomes a live, ongoing practice rather than a one-off signature. In social care, closely related [telecare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecare) — alarms and sensors that help people stay safe at home — is owned by Chapter 9.3 (Digital in Adult Social Care).

## Best practices

1. **Choose the modality from clinical appropriateness, not convenience or target.** The first question is never "can we do this remotely?" but "should we, for this patient, this time?" Some presentations need hands, eyes and a room — a possible acute abdomen, a safeguarding concern, a frail patient whose deterioration shows in the walk from the waiting room. Build explicit criteria for when each channel is and is not appropriate, empower any clinician to convert a remote contact to face-to-face without friction or penalty, and treat "remote-first" as a default to be overridden on clinical judgement, not a quota to be hit.

2. **Design the whole access pathway, not just the front door.** Total triage and digital-first access only work when every route in — online form, phone, walk-in, carer request — lands in one assessment process with consistent clinical prioritization and a guaranteed non-digital alternative. Map what happens after the form is submitted: who reviews it, in what time, with what safety-netting for the patient who deteriorates while waiting. A digital front door that quietly lengthens the path for the sickest or the least digitally confident is a safety and equity failure, however slick the interface (see Chapter 3.3 — Patient & Citizen-Facing Digital Services).

3. **Make clinical accountability for monitored data explicit and resourced.** The single most dangerous pattern in remote care is data that is collected but not watched. Every monitoring stream needs a named clinical owner, a defined review frequency, clear thresholds that trigger action, and an escalation route that works out of hours — and the staffing to sustain all of it. Be honest about the difference between "monitored" and "watched": a dashboard checked twice a day is a different clinical promise from continuous surveillance, and patients and referrers must know which they are getting (see Chapter 1.6 — Clinical Safety & Risk Management).

4. **Build the virtual ward's operating model before the technology.** A virtual ward is a clinical service with admission and discharge criteria, a named consultant or senior clinical lead, a multidisciplinary team, daily board rounds, and a rapid-response route when a patient deteriorates. Decide the staffing model, the caseload per clinician, the visiting logistics, and the interface with the acute site — including how a patient is readmitted physically when home care is no longer safe. The monitoring kit and app are the easy part; the rota, the escalation pathway and the clinical governance are what make it a ward rather than a set of gadgets (see Chapter 7.2 — Workforce Planning).

5. **Design for digital inclusion from the start, and measure who you exclude.** Assume that a meaningful share of your patients lack a suitable device, reliable connectivity, data allowance, digital confidence, or the language and literacy to use your service — and that these are often the people in greatest clinical need. Offer assisted-digital and non-digital routes as first-class options, lend equipment where appropriate, and monitor uptake and outcomes broken down by age, deprivation, disability and ethnicity so you can see whether the service is widening or narrowing inequality (see Chapter 1.9 — Digital Inclusion & Accessibility). An unmeasured remote service is presumed to be excluding someone; find out who.

6. **Treat consent, privacy and safeguarding in the home as continuous practice.** Bringing cameras, microphones and sensors into a home changes the privacy contract profoundly: other household members are captured, the setting reveals things a clinic never would, and the patient may feel unable to refuse. Make consent specific and revocable — what is collected, who sees it, how long it is kept — and design for the reality that a video call can expose neglect, coercion or abuse that triggers safeguarding duties (see Chapter 1.10 — Safeguarding in Digital Services, and Chapter 1.8 — Data Ethics, Consent & Public Trust). Give clinicians a protocol for what to do when a remote contact raises a concern the patient did not intend to share.

7. **Engineer the rota and workflow for remote work as a distinct discipline.** Remote consultation and monitoring are not the same job done from a different chair; they demand different skills, different fatigue patterns and different tooling. Design rotas that account for the cognitive load of back-to-back video, the vigilance load of monitoring queues, and the need to blend remote review with in-person visits for virtual-ward patients. Give staff proper kit, connectivity, quiet space, training in remote clinical assessment, and workflow that surfaces the right patient at the right moment rather than burying alerts in an unmanageable list.

8. **Evaluate against safety, experience and outcomes — not just activity.** It is tempting to report a remote service by counting contacts, but volume says nothing about whether it is safe, fair or effective. Track clinical outcomes and safety incidents, patient and carer experience, equity of access and outcome across groups, did-not-attend (DNA) and technology-failure rates, escalation and readmission rates from virtual wards, and staff experience. Compare against the pathway you replaced, publish what you find, and be willing to retire or redesign a remote model that is not delivering (see Chapter 8.6 — Research & Real-World Evaluation, and Chapter 6.2 — Benefits Realization & Value Management).

## Questions to discuss with your team

1. **For this pathway, where is the line between what is clinically appropriate to do remotely and what is not — and who is allowed to override it?** This matters because the pressure to move care remotely often comes from capacity and cost, while the judgement about whether it is safe for a given patient sits with the clinician in the moment. Debate the concrete cases: which presentations, which patient groups, which risk factors should never default to remote, and what red flags should force a face-to-face conversion. Surface the tension between a "remote-first" target and a clinician's discretion — if converting to in-person is slow, penalized, or invisible in the metrics, staff will feel pressured to keep patients on the wrong channel. Consider how the decision differs for a first contact versus a follow-up, and for a patient the clinician knows well versus a stranger. A good, honest answer produces explicit appropriateness criteria, a frictionless route to escalate to face-to-face, and agreement that clinical judgement overrides operational default every time — with no one second-guessed for using it.

2. **Who is our remote service quietly excluding, and how would we even know?** Digital-first and remote models risk widening inequality precisely because the people least able to use them — older, poorer, disabled, isolated, or less digitally confident patients — are often those in greatest need. Discuss how your access data is broken down: can you see uptake, DNA rates and outcomes by age, deprivation, disability, ethnicity and language, or only a single headline number that hides the gaps? Probe the assisted-digital and non-digital routes: are they genuinely equivalent first-class options, or a grudging afterthought that adds delay and stigma? Consider the hardest cases — the patient with no smartphone, no data, no English, or no private space for a video call — and whether your service reaches them at all. A good answer names the groups at risk, commits to measuring outcomes not just access, and treats an equity gap as a defect to fix rather than an acceptable cost of efficiency.

3. **What exactly is our promise about the data we monitor — and is it resourced to be true?** The most dangerous gap in remote care is between what patients and referrers *think* is happening to their readings and what the service is actually staffed to do. Debate the precise clinical promise for each monitoring stream: is it continuous surveillance, twice-daily review, or a patient-triggered alert model — and does everyone in the chain understand which? Examine the out-of-hours reality: who watches the data at 2am, what happens when a reading breaches a threshold, and how fast can a deteriorating patient be reached or readmitted. Surface the resourcing honestly — a monitoring service without the staff to act on what it sees is not a safety net but a liability, and the incident review after a missed deterioration will ask who owned the number. A good answer states the promise plainly, matches it to a named owner and a funded rota, and closes any gap between the reassurance given and the response guaranteed.

4. **When a virtual-ward patient deteriorates at home, what exactly happens — and have we rehearsed it?** A virtual ward is only as safe as its worst-case pathway, yet the escalation and physical-readmission route is often the least designed part of the model. Walk through a concrete deterioration: who is contacted, in what order, how quickly a clinician can reach the home or bring the patient back to an acute bed, and what happens if that route is saturated on a winter evening. Surface the interfaces that tend to fail — the handover between the community team and the acute site, the ambulance conveyance that competes with the wider system, the out-of-hours gap when the board round is over and the caseload is still live. Consider whether your admission criteria are honest about which patients this ward can safely hold, and whether the caseload cap protects the response time or merely the headline capacity number. An honest answer names the escalation route step by step, states the time within which a patient can be readmitted, confirms it has been tested rather than assumed, and admits which patient types the ward should not be taking at all.

5. **How do we hold consent, privacy and safeguarding as a living practice once care is inside someone's home?** Putting cameras, microphones and sensors into a household crosses a threshold a waiting room never does, and the people affected include those who never consented — other residents, carers, children in the background. Discuss how consent is made specific and revocable in practice: does the patient genuinely understand what is captured, who sees it, and how to switch it off, or is it a one-off signature at enrolment that no one revisits? Probe the safeguarding reality — a video call can reveal neglect, coercion, self-neglect or a carer at breaking point that the patient did not intend to show — and whether clinicians have a clear protocol for acting on what they see rather than recording it and moving on. Weigh the tension between the clinical value of richer home data and the intrusion it represents, especially for patients who feel unable to refuse a service they depend on. A good answer treats consent as ongoing and reversible, equips staff to respond to incidental safeguarding concerns, and is explicit about the privacy of everyone the monitoring touches, not just the patient.

6. **Are we honestly willing to redesign or retire this remote model if the evidence says it is not working?** Remote and virtual services are frequently launched with political and capacity momentum behind them, which makes them hard to question once activity is flowing — but volume is not value, and a service that shifts activity without improving safety, equity or outcomes is consuming resource it cannot justify. Debate what evidence would actually change your mind: which outcome, safety, experience and equity measures you would track, against what comparison to the pathway you replaced, and what result would trigger a redesign or a stop. Examine the organizational honesty this demands — publishing an equity gap that is not yet closed, admitting a virtual ward is not freeing the capacity claimed, resisting the pull to keep a funded programme alive for its own sake. Consider who owns that decision and whether the evaluation is independent enough to deliver bad news. An honest answer commits to a real evaluation plan with pre-agreed thresholds for action, names who can call time on the model, and treats the willingness to stop as a mark of a safe service rather than a failure.

## In practice: a health & care example

Wyvern Vale Integrated Care System runs a frailty and acute respiratory virtual ward across two acute trusts and a community provider, aiming to care for up to 200 patients at home who would otherwise occupy hospital beds. Early enthusiasm produced a pilot that looked good on an activity dashboard — hundreds of patients enrolled — but a serious incident forced a rethink: a patient's oxygen readings had drifted downward over a weekend, no one had been rostered to review the overnight queue, and the deterioration was caught late.

The ICS rebuilds the service around the operating model rather than the app. It sets clear admission and discharge criteria, appoints a named consultant clinical lead per virtual ward, and defines the staffing: a daily virtual board round, a caseload cap per clinician, community nurses for in-person visits, and a rapid-response route to readmit a patient physically within a defined time when home care is no longer safe. Crucially, it makes the monitoring promise explicit — patients are told this is twice-daily structured review plus a patient-triggered alert line, not continuous surveillance — and it funds a rota that actually covers evenings and weekends, with a single clinician owning the escalation queue at all times.

Access and equity get equal attention. The team finds that early enrolment skewed towards patients who already owned smartphones and lived in less deprived areas, so it introduces loaned monitoring kit with pre-paid connectivity, a telephone-only monitoring option for those who cannot use the app, and translated onboarding with carer involvement. Consent is reworked to be specific and revocable, and staff get a safeguarding protocol for when a home video reveals a concern — after a video call surfaces a carer under visible strain, the team is able to route it to the right support rather than record it and move on.

Six months on, Wyvern Vale evaluates against outcomes, not counts. It reports escalation and readmission rates, patient and carer experience, safety incidents, technology-failure rates, and uptake broken down by deprivation and age — and it finds the equity gap narrowing but not closed, which it publishes rather than hides. The virtual ward is now genuinely freeing acute capacity, but the leadership is clear that it earned that outcome through the rota, the escalation pathway and the inclusion work, not through the monitoring devices, which were never the hard part.

## Sector lenses

### Startup

A digital-health startup offering a remote-consultation or monitoring platform lives or dies on whether clinicians and commissioners trust its clinical safety and evidence, not on feature count. Its ways of working must build in clinical safety governance, a real-world evidence plan, and honest positioning about what the product does and does not clinically promise — resisting the temptation to imply surveillance it cannot deliver. A small company can move fast on usability and integration, which is a genuine edge, but it must design for digital inclusion and accessibility from the outset because an NHS buyer will test exactly that. The credible startup sells not just software but a defensible answer to "who is accountable when a monitored reading is missed?"

### Small business

An established small provider — a GP practice, a community pharmacy, a single-site clinic, a care home or a domiciliary-care agency — is expected to offer remote consultation or monitoring under the same clinical-safety, consent and equity duties as a large trust, but with no dedicated IT or governance team to carry them. The realistic move is to adopt tried, well-supported tools and national frameworks rather than build or heavily customize, and to be ruthless about which remote services it can genuinely resource to be safe: a monitoring stream it cannot staff to watch out of hours is one it should not offer. Its advantage is proximity — it knows its patients, their homes and their exclusions personally, so digital inclusion and a guaranteed non-digital route can be handled as a relationship rather than a policy. The binding constraints are staff time and the absence of an out-of-hours rota, so it should keep the clinical promise modest and explicit, and lean on shared or federated arrangements (a primary care network, a supplier's clinical support) for escalation cover it cannot provide alone.

### Enterprise

A large NHS trust or ICS running virtual wards and total triage at scale faces a coordination and workforce challenge more than a technology one. The realistic goal is a consistent operating model across sites — shared admission criteria, escalation pathways, staffing standards and evaluation metrics — rather than a patchwork of local pilots that each invent their own safety net. It must solve the hard interfaces: between the virtual ward and the acute site, between digital-first access and the patients who cannot use it, and between monitoring queues and the rotas that staff them. At this scale the biggest risks are unowned monitoring data and an access model that widens inequality, so governance, workforce planning and equity measurement matter more than any individual app.

### Government

A national body — NHS England, NICE, a devolved health department — shapes whether remote care is safe, fair and evidence-based across the whole system. Its leverage is to set operating standards and clinical-safety expectations for virtual wards and total triage, fund and evaluate models rigorously, and mandate equity monitoring so that digital-first access does not quietly entrench the digital divide. It carries the evidence duty: commissioning real-world evaluation that tells the system which remote models actually improve outcomes and which merely shift activity, and being willing to say so. Government also holds the equity backstop — ensuring statutory rights to access are preserved for people who cannot or will not use digital channels (see Chapter 9.1 — Working with Local & National Governments).

## Common failure modes

- **Monitored but not watched.** Readings stream in, but no one is resourced to review them, especially out of hours. Assign a named owner, a review frequency, thresholds and a funded escalation rota — or do not collect the data.
- **Remote by target, not by judgement.** A "remote-first" quota pressures clinicians to keep unsuitable patients on the wrong channel. Make face-to-face conversion frictionless and invisible in the metrics that penalize it.
- **A digital front door that excludes.** Total triage that assumes everyone can complete an online form lengthens the path for the sickest and least confident. Guarantee an equivalent non-digital route and measure who uses which.
- **The app is the service.** Buying monitoring kit without building the rota, escalation pathway and governance produces gadgets, not a virtual ward. Design the operating model first.
- **Consent as a signature.** Treating home monitoring consent as a one-off tick ignores the ongoing intrusion into a household. Make it specific, revocable and revisited, and plan for safeguarding concerns a home view can reveal.
- **Activity mistaken for value.** Counting contacts says nothing about safety, equity or outcome. Evaluate against clinical outcomes, experience, equity and escalation — and compare to the pathway replaced.
- **Equity unmeasured.** A single headline uptake figure hides who is being left behind. Break access and outcomes down by deprivation, age, disability, ethnicity and language.

## Maturity model

| Dimension | Initiate | Develop | Standardize | Manage | Orchestrate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Clinical appropriateness | Remote used ad hoc or by blanket default | Some guidance on when remote is suitable | Explicit criteria per pathway; frictionless conversion to face-to-face | Conversion rates and appropriateness breaches tracked against targets and reviewed | Continuously reviewed criteria informed by outcome and incident data |
| Accountability for data | Data collected with no clear owner | Owner named but review inconsistent | Named owner, thresholds, escalation and funded rota, out of hours covered | Review timeliness and missed-threshold events measured and assured against KPIs | Accountability audited; promise to patients matched to staffed response |
| Virtual ward operating model | Technology-led pilot, unclear staffing | Basic criteria and daytime staffing | Admission/discharge criteria, clinical lead, MDT, escalation, board rounds | Caseload, escalation times and readmission rates monitored and managed to target | Model standardized, benchmarked, and continuously improved across sites |
| Digital inclusion | No non-digital route; exclusion unmeasured | Alternative routes exist but are afterthoughts | Assisted/non-digital routes as first-class; access measured by group | Uptake and outcome gaps by group tracked against equity targets and actioned | Outcomes (not just access) equitable across groups and actively improved |
| Evaluation | Activity counts only | Some experience and safety data | Outcomes, experience, equity, escalation and DNA tracked and reviewed | Metrics governed against defined thresholds that trigger redesign decisions | Findings published, benchmarked, and used to retire or redesign models |

## Checklist

- [ ] Each pathway has explicit criteria for when remote care is and is not clinically appropriate, and a frictionless route to convert to face-to-face.
- [ ] Every monitoring stream has a named clinical owner, defined review frequency, action thresholds, and an escalation route that works out of hours.
- [ ] The clinical promise about monitored data (continuous vs periodic vs alert-based) is explicit to patients and referrers, and staffed to be true.
- [ ] Virtual wards have documented admission and discharge criteria, a named clinical lead, a multidisciplinary team, and a rapid physical-readmission route.
- [ ] Total triage routes every request through one assessment with a guaranteed, equivalent non-digital alternative.
- [ ] Assisted-digital and non-digital access routes are offered as first-class options, with equipment or connectivity provided where needed.
- [ ] Uptake, DNA, and outcomes are measured broken down by age, deprivation, disability, ethnicity and language.
- [ ] Consent for home monitoring is specific, revocable and revisited, and a safeguarding protocol exists for concerns a remote contact reveals.
- [ ] Rotas and workflow are designed for the distinct load of remote consultation and monitoring, with proper kit, space and training.
- [ ] The service is evaluated against safety, experience, outcomes, equity and escalation — not activity alone — and compared to the pathway it replaced.
